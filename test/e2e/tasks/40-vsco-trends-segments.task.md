Test whether consumer search interest is a useful early signal for Victoria's Secret &
Co (VSCO) sales. VSCO is a hard case on purpose: it is a highly seasonal retailer on
the retail (4-5-4) fiscal calendar — its fiscal year ends in early February and its
fourth quarter contains the holiday season — so naive calendar alignment or raw-level
comparison will produce nonsense.

1. Fetch Google Trends search interest (worldwide or US) for "Victoria's Secret" and,
   separately, for "PINK Victoria's Secret", covering the last 3 years. Aggregate the
   weekly/monthly interest into VSCO's retail fiscal quarters (state the month-to-quarter
   mapping you used). Google Trends data is required for this task — do not substitute
   another attention proxy such as Wikipedia pageviews.
2. From VSCO's quarterly earnings releases or 10-Q/10-K disclosures, extract quarterly
   net sales and the finest revenue split the company reports (by channel — e.g. Stores
   North America, Direct, International — or by brand if disclosed).
3. Seasonality dominates this business, so compare year-over-year changes of fiscal
   quarters against the same quarter a year earlier — never sequential quarters and
   never raw levels of an interest index against dollars.

Deliver a single report with: one chart of search-interest YoY change vs net-sales YoY
change on the aligned fiscal-quarter axis, one chart of the reported revenue split over
the period, and a short written verdict on whether search interest led, coincided with,
or was useless for predicting VSCO's reported sales — with the strongest example quarter
for whichever conclusion you reach. Cite all sources.

Assemble the final deliverable with the reporting skill: a branded PDF report written to `output/40-vsco-trends-segments.pdf`.
